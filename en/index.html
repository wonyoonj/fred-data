How to Interpret the Data?

The main screen animations illustrate the 'weekly net changes' of fund flows among economic entities. Here is what the numbers mean:

  • Treasury General Account (TGA) Balance (- Negative): Indicates the government is injecting money into the markets by drawing down cash or slowing bond issuance. (Liquidity Injection)
  • Treasury General Account (TGA) Balance (+ Positive): Means the government is absorbing cash from the market via tax collections or expanding net bond issuances. (Liquidity Absorption)

  • FED Net Liquidity Change (- Negative): The Federal Reserve is removing money via Quantitative Tightening (asset roll-offs), or institutions are parking cash in Reverse Repos. (Liquidity Absorption)
  • FED Net Liquidity Change (+ Positive): The FED is actively providing money via asset purchases (QE), or institutions are pulling cash out of Reverse Repos back into the economy. (Liquidity Injection)

  • MMF → FED Flow (+ Positive): Money Market Funds are depositing more cash into the FED's Reverse Repo Facility (RRP), taking liquidity out of circulation.
  • MMF → FED Flow (- Negative): MMFs are withdrawing funds from the RRP facility, turning it loose into market channels.

  • MMF → Market Flow (+ Positive): MMFs are actively buying market assets like short-term Treasury bills or commercial paper, adding liquid capital.
  • MMF → Market Flow (- Negative): Market participants are shifting capital out of investments and into MMF cash accounts, drawing liquidity from broader markets.

In summary, if the Net Market Flow entering the market is blue and negative (-), market liquidity is draining. Conversely, if it is red and positive (+), market liquidity is expanding.

Interest Rate Indicators Guide

  • Reverse Repo (RRP) Rate: The rate paid by the FED to financial institutions to deposit cash overnight, setting a soft floor for market rates.
  • Discount Window Rate: The interest rate charged to commercial banks for short-term emergency loans from the Federal Reserve.
  • Effective Federal Funds Rate (EFFR): The volume-weighted median rate of overnight unsecured loans between commercial banks.
  • Secured Overnight Financing Rate (SOFR): A broad measure of the cost of borrowing cash overnight secured by Treasury securities.
  • 3-Month Treasury Yield: Reflects short-term government funding costs and near-term monetary policy paths.
  • 2-Year Treasury Yield: Highly sensitive to mid-term interest rate and economic expectations.
  • 10-Year Treasury Yield: Serves as a global economic benchmark for mortgage and corporate lending rates, signaling long-term economic expansion and inflation.
  • SOFR-EFFR Spread: Measures the premium difference between secured and unsecured funding, highlighting liquidity strains when widening.
  • US Treasury Yield Curve (10Y-2Y): A historic leading indicator for economic recessions when it inverts below zero.

Dollar Liquidity Drives Stock Valuations

In 2022, as the FED's Reverse Repo balances expanded sharply, the S&P 500 declined into a bear market. The direction and velocity of net currency flows are becoming increasingly central to global asset behaviors. This dashboard aims to simplify liquidity predictions to help optimize your equity portfolios.
